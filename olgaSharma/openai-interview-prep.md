## The Three Required Topics

### What is an LLM?

**Your definition (say it simply):**

"A large language model is an AI system that's been trained on a huge amount of text, basically most of the internet, so it can understand and generate human language. OpenAI's models, like GPT-5 and GPT-5 mini, are LLMs. They can write, summarize, translate, answer questions, classify content, and reason through problems. The 'large' in the name refers to the billions of patterns the model has learned, which is what makes it so versatile."

**If they ask you to explain it to a customer:**

"Think of it as the most well-read colleague you've ever worked with. It's processed billions of documents and can now help with anything language-based. The difference from traditional software is that you give it instructions in plain English, not code. You say 'summarize this customer support thread in three bullet points' and it does it. That's what makes it accessible to non-technical teams."

**If they ask for an application example:**

"Take Discord. Every time a moderator manually reads through a thousand messages to summarize what happened overnight, an LLM can do that in seconds. Every time a user asks a question that was already answered two weeks ago, an LLM can search past conversations and surface the answer automatically. It turns unstructured conversation data into structured, searchable knowledge."

---

### What is prompt engineering?

**Your definition:**

"Prompt engineering is the practice of writing better instructions for the AI to get better results. It's like the difference between telling a new hire 'handle the quarterly report' versus 'write a two-page summary of Q3 results focusing on revenue growth, new customers acquired, and churn rate, formatted with bullet points and written for a board audience.' Same person, completely different output."

**If they ask why it matters for customers:**

"It matters because it's the cheapest, fastest way to improve AI output quality. Before a customer invests in fine-tuning or custom model training, they should exhaust prompt engineering first. Adding specific constraints to a prompt, things like output length, format, tone, or what to prioritize, solves about 80% of quality complaints at zero additional cost. It's the first thing I'd work on with a Solutions Engineer when a customer says their results aren't good enough."

**If they ask for an example:**

"A customer says their AI-generated summaries are too long and too generic. Instead of suggesting they fine-tune a model, which takes weeks and costs real money, I'd start by refining the prompt. Something like: 'Summarize in exactly three bullet points, fifteen words maximum each, prioritize decisions and action items, skip anything that's just casual conversation.' That kind of precision in the prompt usually fixes the problem immediately."

---

### What is fine-tuning, and when should it be used?

**Your definition:**

"Fine-tuning is when you take one of OpenAI's base models and train it further on your own data so it specializes in your specific use case. Right now it's available for GPT-4.1 and GPT-4.1 mini. You're teaching the model your company's patterns: your terminology, your tone, your standards. After fine-tuning, the model performs that specific task more consistently and often with shorter prompts, because it already knows the context."

**When to use it (the hierarchy):**

"I think of it as a progression. Start with prompt engineering. It's free, fast, and iterative. If that doesn't get you consistent enough results, try RAG (giving the model your own documents as context). If you've tried both and still need more specialization, that's when fine-tuning makes sense. It's more expensive and takes longer, but the payoff is a model that really understands your domain."

**If they ask for an example:**

"Back to Discord. A gaming server and an enterprise developer community have very different moderation standards. What's acceptable trash talk in a gaming server would be completely unprofessional in a corporate channel. You could try writing separate prompts for each context, but at Discord's scale with millions of servers, fine-tuning makes more sense. Train one model on gaming community norms and another on enterprise norms. Each one applies the right standards automatically without needing long, complex prompts."

**Revenue angle (if it comes up):**

"From a revenue perspective, fine-tuning is interesting because it generates revenue twice. First, the training itself costs money, about $25 per million tokens. Second, once the fine-tuned model is deployed, every time it runs in production, it generates consumption revenue. So it's an expansion lever within an account."

## Bonus Concepts

Know these well enough to explain them if they come up. You don't need to volunteer them, but if an interviewer mentions any of these, you should be able to hold a conversation.

**Structured Outputs:** A feature that guarantees the model's response matches a specific format you define (called a JSON schema). Why customers care: instead of getting free-form text that your app has to parse and hope is formatted correctly, you get guaranteed structure. Every field is exactly where you expect it. This matters for any customer building AI into their product, because their software needs predictable inputs.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation):** Instead of asking the model to answer from its general training data (which can lead to made-up answers, called hallucination), you first pull in relevant documents from the customer's own data, then ask the model to answer based only on that. It's like giving someone a reference binder before asking them a question. Much more accurate. Customers use RAG when they need answers grounded in their specific data: internal wikis, help docs, past conversations.

**Embeddings:** A way to convert text into numbers that capture meaning. Two sentences with similar meaning get similar numbers, even if they use different words. This powers semantic search, which means searching by meaning instead of exact keyword matches. If a Discord user asks "how do I change my avatar?" and the answer was previously given as "here's how to update your profile picture," embeddings help the system realize those are the same question.

**Tokens:** How OpenAI charges. One token is roughly three-quarters of a word. Both the input (what you send to the model) and the output (what the model generates) count toward cost. GPT-5 mini costs $0.25 per million input tokens. GPT-5 costs $1.25. Understanding tokens is important for helping customers estimate costs and choose the right model for each task.

**The GPT model family (current as of March 2026):**

- GPT-5.4: $2.50/$15 per million tokens. Latest, most capable. 1M token context window. Released March 5, 2026.
- GPT-5: $1.25/$10. Reasoning model. 400K context.
- GPT-5 mini: $0.25/$2. Cost-efficient workhorse for high-volume tasks.
- GPT-5 nano: $0.05 input. Cheapest, simplest tasks.
- GPT-4.1: $2/$8. Non-reasoning model, 1M context. Supports fine-tuning.

**Agents SDK:** A newer framework for building AI agents that chain multiple tools together in automated workflows. Think of it as: instead of making one API call at a time, you build an agent that can call the Moderation API, then the Embeddings API, then GPT-5 mini in sequence, all orchestrated automatically. Customers use this to build complex AI-powered workflows without writing all the orchestration code themselves.

**Consumption revenue:** OpenAI's business model. Customers pay based on how much they use (tokens processed), not a flat subscription. More usage means more revenue. As an Account Director, your job is to help customers deploy AI successfully so their usage grows. You don't renegotiate the contract for more money. You help them find more ways to use the product, and the revenue follows.

# THINGS NOT TO SAY (across all four sessions)

- Don't say "I'm not technical" without immediately following it with a technical point. Say "I'm not an engineer, but..." and then demonstrate reasoning.
- Don't say "GPT-4o" or "GPT-4." Those are previous generation. The current models are GPT-5, GPT-5 mini, and GPT-5.4 (pronounced "GPT five-four").
- Don't claim fine-tuning works on GPT-5 models. It doesn't yet. Fine-tuning is available on GPT-4.1 and GPT-4.1 mini.
- Don't oversell. Don't say "this will definitely work." Say "based on comparable deployments" or "comparable implementations show."
- Don't badmouth Meta. Don't say "Meta was too slow" or "Meta didn't value partnerships." Frame your departure positively.
- Don't badmouth competitors. Say "OpenAI has a strong advantage here" not "Anthropic can't do that."
- Don't quote exact dollar amounts for OpenAI deal sizes. Say "multi-million dollar" and let them size it.
- Don't confuse the Realtime API with Whisper. Whisper transcribes recorded audio. The Realtime API handles live streaming audio. They're complementary.
